Notes, octaves & keys

Pitch is logarithmic. Western music slices each octave — a doubling of frequency — into 12 equal steps. That's the whole foundation; everything else is which steps you pick.

The octave & equal temperament

An octave is a 2:1 frequency ratio: A4 = 440 Hz, A5 = 880 Hz, A3 = 220 Hz — the "same note", doubled. 12-tone equal temperament divides that ratio into 12 equal multiplicative steps, so one semitone = the twelfth root of two:

ratio per semitone = 21/12 ≈ 1.05946  ·  f(n) = 440 · 2(n−69)/12 Hz  (n = MIDI number)

Equal steps in log frequency = equal musical intervals. (Just intonation uses small whole-number ratios like 3:2; equal temperament trades pure ratios for the freedom to play in every key — a classic engineering compromise.)

Seven "natural" notes (white keys) C D E F G A B and five "accidentals" (black keys). A black key has two names — C♯ = D♭ — these enharmonic spellings sound identical in equal temperament but mean different things in a key.

A key = a tonic + a scale

A key picks a home note (the tonic) and a 7-note pattern from the 12. Major = bright; minor = darker. There are 12 of each (15 written, counting enharmonic spellings like F♯/G♭ major).

The "comb". Because a key is a fixed, octave-repeating subset of the 12 pitches, locking a synth to it means you literally cannot pick a wrong note — the gaps where wrong notes live are unreachable. That's the safety net behind The Black Theater.
The circle of fifths

Stack perfect fifths (×3/2, the strongest consonance after the octave) and after 12 steps you cycle through all 12 notes and back home. Order them in a circle and adjacent keys differ by just one sharp/flat — which is why it organizes key signatures and chord relationships.

The modes

Play the white keys from C to C and you get the major scale. Start and end on a different white key — same notes, new home — and the pattern of steps rotates, giving a different color. Those seven rotations are the diatonic modes.

The seven modes — brightest to darkest · click to hear (root A)

2 = whole step (tone)   1 = half step (semitone). The position of the half-steps is what gives each mode its character.

Brightness is literally how many notes are raised vs. lowered relative to the others. Lydian (raised 4th) is the brightest; Locrian (flat 2nd and flat 5th) the darkest. Phrygian sits second-darkest — dark enough to feel ancient, but with a stable home, unlike the unusable-as-a-home Locrian.

Where the names come from

The modal names are ancient Greek, after peoples and regions around the Aegean and Anatolia. Medieval theorists borrowed the Greek terms for the church modes — and, famously, misassigned them: the scale the ancient Greeks called Phrygian is not the one we call Phrygian today. The modern set of names was finally codified by Heinrich Glarean in his Dodecachordon (1547).

Phrygian — the black of the theater

The mode we chose for the piece. Here is exactly what it is, why it sounds the way it does, and the strange history behind its name.

What it is

Phrygian is the third mode — the white keys from E to E, or transposed to our root, A Phrygian: A B♭ C D E F G. Its step pattern leads with a half step:

1222122

Play them back to back. The only difference between Phrygian and natural minor is one note — the second degree is lowered (B → B♭). That single flat-2nd, just a semitone above the tonic, is the whole character.

Why it sounds dark

That ♭2 a half-step above the home note is the most tense interval in the scale, and it's right next to "home" — so the tension never resolves comfortably. The ear reads it as ancient, foreboding, "Eastern" or flamenco (Spanish music leans hard on Phrygian and its raised-3rd cousin, the Phrygian dominant). It's dark without being unusable: unlike Locrian, the 5th is still perfect, so the tonic chord is stable enough to rest on. Perfect for a vast, lonely, slightly threatening space — a black theater full of eyes.

The naming twist. The ancient Greek "Phrygian" tónos was, in its diatonic octave species, what we'd now call Dorian. Medieval theorists (via Boethius) reapplied the Greek names to a different system, so the modern Phrygian is a historical reassignment, not a continuation of the Greek one. The name traveled from a people in Anatolia, through a thousand years of misattribution, to the flat-2nd scale we use today.

Why we chose it

"Make the key the black of the theater." Phrygian is the darkest mode you can still build a home in — and over an open-fifth drone (root + 5th, no third), the harmony stays "black" and undecided, while that ♭2 (the B♭ in our envy cluster) supplies the unease of being watched. The mode is the dream's mood, encoded.

Electronic & algorithmic music

Two ideas underpin this whole project. Electronic music makes sound from circuits and code instead of vibrating wood and string. Algorithmic music composes by rules and processes instead of placing every note by hand. Our pieces are both.

The signal flow

The classic synth voice — and ours — is a chain, with modulators bending it over time:

OSCILLATOR → FILTER → AMPLIFIER → EFFECTS
    ↑ pitch     ↑ cutoff   ↑ volume
modulated by ENVELOPES (ADSR) & LFOs

An envelope shapes one event in time (attack→decay→sustain→release); an LFO is a slow oscillator that wobbles a parameter (vibrato, tremolo, filter sweeps). Effects — reverb, delay, distortion — add space, time, and grit.

Algorithmic music — composing with rules

Instead of writing every note, you define a process and let it generate the music. Crucially, a seed makes randomness reproducible — the same seed always yields the same "random" result, so a generative piece is repeatable, like any deterministic system.
